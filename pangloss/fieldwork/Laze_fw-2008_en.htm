Study of the Laze language (2008)

 

In the Spring of 2008, I went to the Tibetan county of Muli 木里 (prefecture of Liangshan 凉山, province: Sichuan 四川) with Katia Chirkova, who heads a research project about the languages of this county. The purpose of this stay was to begin the study of a language of Muli county that has similarities with the Naxi (=Na, Mosuo/Moso) languages that I have been studying since 2002. Muli neighbours on Ninglang, a county where I stayed several times (village of Yongning).

After careful consideration about the language variety to study, the choice pitched on a dialect whose autonym is [la.ze] (analysed phonemically as /la.zi/). To our knowledge, this language variety is undocumented so far, apart from data collected by Katia Chirkova during one of her previous stays in Muli. The brief mention of the Laze in the historical book by Guo Dalie et He Zhiwu, A History of the Naxi People (in Chinese; 1992 [2nd ed. 1999]), where their name is approximated in Chinese characters as 拉热, suggested that Laze had strong similarities with Na/Naxi. Laze is spoken in the district of Xiangjiao 项脚, relatively close to the town of Muli.

我 在2008年春天首次到四川凉山木里臧族自治县与Katia Chirkova(齐卡佳)合作调查这一地区的语言。很感谢中国中央社会科学院民族研究所与当地政府的 帮助，让我们顺利完成工作计划。我这次的研究重点是着手调查一个方言，自称为[la.ze] (音位分析为/la.zi/)，汉名”拉热“（见 郭大烈，和志武 1994， 纳西族史， 重庆: 四川民族出版社， 第6页）。讲” 拉热“语的族群分布在木里县项脚蒙古族乡。

Daily work sessions with one language consultant allowed for the collection of a word list (some 2,000 entries), sentences elicited via Mandarin Chinese, and narratives. These data were partly verified with a second speaker. The tools used for recording depend on the aim of the recording: audio for the entire corpus; electroglottography for the study of tones; a 'nasal microphone' (ferromagnetic transducer) for the study of nasal vowels and consonants.

These first data already reveal strong similarities between Laze and Naxi dialects spoken further to the South. Thanks to the linguistic acumen of the main consultant, this first approach allows for a phonemic inventory of the language and for numerous insights into its tonal system. The transcribed narratives offer a basis for future morphosyntactic research, as well as for phonetic/phonological reflections on continuous speech.

The period during which fieldwork took place was rather eventful. There did not remain any visible trace of the damage caused by the exceptional snowfalls of this Winter; on the other hand, the earthquake in Sichuan occasioned some emotion for our small team, as well as for our family, friends and colleagues. Fortunately, Muli did not suffer from the earthquake.

The events in Tibet in March also had major consequences. Locally, Muli, as an autonomous Tibetan county, was the object of much attention; thanks to the support of the partners of the project, we were able to complete our stay as originally planned.
