Paicî corpus

Paicî: a brief note

 

The child who came out of a tree
New Caledonia, Galilée, 1967

“The child who came out of a tree” was told in 1967 by Irénée Kowi, a Paicî speaker living in Galilée village, in the Amoa Valley. The text of this story, transcribed and translated, figures in the volume by Alban Bensa and J.-C. Rivierre, Histoire canaques. The version presented here was slightly modified so as to follow the speaker’s narrative as closely as possible. Tonal notation and a word by word translation have been added. In this text “One sees an ancestor place among the people a child who had been living in a tree. The child, like someone returned from the dead, lives among the people but without really belonging to their world: his phantasmagoric and supernatural essence is indicated by his mysterious ability to disappear into the trunk of a tree at nightfall. The last rays of sun tell the master of the spot of his presence. But the old man’s magic tears the child away from the plant world: he is adopted. Contradictory references suggest that the child is able to occupy different social positions:
- Born of a tree standing in the main passage, near the ancestor’s burial mound, he could be the symbol for autochthones, founders, as deeply rooted in their land as the trees that grow there.
- Being signaled by the setting sun’s rays, one could consider the child a stranger, the last comer waiting in the bush for the founders to accept to take him in. (…) The account of the origins takes on an aspect of a simple parable, the enacting of an essential but ever problematic social process: the integration of a new arrival.” [in Bensa A. and Rivierre J.-C., Histoires canaques, Paris, CILF, 1983, pp. 8-9]

 

Jean-Claude Rivierre
