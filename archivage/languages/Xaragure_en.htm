A Traditional Speech on Wood
New Caledonia, Thio, 1980, Claire Moyse-Faurie

Ceremonial discourse. This harangue which precedes the “discourse on wood” (xwâxa) is a call for people to gather for the dance.

The River Nèfasia Massacre
New Caledonia, Grand-Borindi, 1980, Claire Moyse-Faurie

This narrative tells the sad story of an episode in the war between the Europeans and the Kanaks in the Thio-Borindi region. Treachery (obtained by blackmail) is supposed to have caused the near extermination of the members of the former Koum tribe (located in the central mountain range), who are said to have been shot down by the Europeans at the mouth of the Nèfasia river.

Maratâ's Answer
New Caledonia, Petit-Borindi, 1980, Claire Moyse-Faurie

This narrative explains how the Borindi Kanaks were dispossessed of the Ngoi lands, through the fault of old Maratâ. The latter, not being quite all there, is said to have wrongly answered the gendarmes who had come to question him.

Mount Bwötémêrê
New Caledonia, Thio, 1977, Claire Moyse-Faurie

This myth, like the one on Kaataamwaroo, raises the delicate problem of chiefs’ daughters’ marriages. Alliances are supposed to be distant and outside the usual rules: the desired husband must belong to the natural world; in this myth, Bwötémêrê is a sea snake. The alliance is successful however, because the eldest sister has Bwötémêrê’s mother come so as to make her son’s exile bearable.

Nyiichaa's Prophecy
New Caledonia, Petit-Borindi, 1980, Claire Moyse-Faurie

This is the story of how the Catholic religion came to Borindi, due to old Nyiichaa’s clairvoyance. The latter predicted that there would be an era of peace if the Fathers were made welcome.

The Story of Kaataamwaroo
New Caledonia, Thio, 1977, Claire Moyse-Faurie

This myth, like that of Bwötémêrê, deals with the delicate problem of chiefs’ daughters’ marriages. Alliances are supposed to be distant and outside the usual rules: Kaataamwaroo is a “pebble”. This type of alliance, where the couple lives in the woman’s town, can only succeed if concessions are made. In this account, the alliance fails and the husband is restored to his original state.

The Three Women from Koum
New Caledonia, Grand-Borindi, 1980, Claire Moyse-Faurie

This short story explains the presence of three unusual rocks, situated half way between the beach and the mountain, between the Ngoi and the Ni rivers, whereas such rocks are usually only encountered in the mountain range, in Koum. They are said to be in fact three women from Koum, who had gone fishing for shells along the sea shore, and who had been overtaken by daylight… Which goes to show that one mustn’t give in to one’s desires for too long!

Trema Leaf and Cherry Tree Leaf
New Caledonia, Thio, 1980, Claire Moyse-Faurie

This story has several variants, but they all have the particularity of constituting a loop. The version presented here shows, beside the two main characters, Trema leaf (Trema vieillardii Schltr., Ulmaceae) and “blue cherry” leaf (Elaeocarpus persicifolius Br. and Gr., Elaeocarpaceae), naughty children who are punished for their misdeeds, before the course of events catches up again. In other versions, the two heroines, Trema and Blue cherry, play tricks on one another, equally scatological. The curiosity of an intruder, Acacia leaf, allows the narrator to go back in time, and the story’s loop can be closed once more.

Young Giröö and His Mother
New Caledonia, Thio, 1980, Claire Moyse-Faurie

In this story, young Giröö saves his mother from being devoured by an ogress. They then get rid of her thanks to the wits and vigilance of the young boy.
