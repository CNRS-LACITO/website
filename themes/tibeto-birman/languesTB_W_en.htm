Languages of the Tibeto-Burman area

Brief survey of recent and current work

  1. Collection and analysis of Tibeto-Burman languages in Nepal, Bhutan, Tibet, China, Assam (India), Pakistan and Burma
    – Grammatical sketches in English of Hayu (pdf) and Tamang (pdf) (Nepal languagesl) were published in 2003. Shorter sketches in French on Limbu (pdf), Tamang (pdf), Tibetan and Burmese were published in 2011.
    – Lexicographical work and corpora have been or are being established for the following languages:

    • Limbu (Nepal). A Limbu dictionary (introduction in pdf) has been published. Limbu texts, with their sound recordings and transcriptions are available on the Internet. An interactive versionof the dictionary is accessible via glossed texts and words.

    • Hayu (Nepal). Texts are available on the Internet.

    • Tamang (Nepal). A dictionary is currently being compiled. Twotexts are available.

    • Kiranti Languages (Nepal). Poursuite de l'étude grammaticale du thulung, esquisse du koyi rai(2009), analyse de la morphologie verbale du khaling (2011) et du dumi (2011).

    • Tibetan. A 2nd edition, revised and enlarged, of the Tibetan Manual by N. Tournadre and S. Dorje has been published. An English version has come out with Snow Lion. A general presentation of the Tibetan dialects will complete the whole.

    • Deuri (Assam). A description has been published (see here in French). Short ethnographic presentation (pdf).

    • Burmese. A thesis on modality in Burmese was defended in 2004: "La modalité et ses corrélats en birman, dans une perspective comparative" [abstract]. Une thèse est en cours portant sur l'énonciation, les fonctions discursives, et la pragmatique en birman, basée sur des techniques de linguistique de corpus.

    • magar : une description d'un dialecte est en cours pour un MA.

    • Naish Languages: Naxi, Na and Laze (Yunnan, China). Etudes tonologiques détaillées. Nombreux textes mis en ligne.

    – Grammaticalization of posture verbs/copula in Limbu
    – Areal typology of Mainland South-East Asia

  6. Languages in contact with Tibeto-Burman languages: 1. Nepali
    Dans le cadre d'un programme ANR "Epopée Népal" une étude pluridisciplinaire réunissant ethnologues, ethno-musicologues et linguistes a sauvé de l'oubli des recueils de tradition chantée datant depuis la fin des années soixante et recueilli, un demi-siècle plus tard, les chants des héritiers de cette tradition dans l'Ouest du Népal. Nombreuses ressources mises sur le web. Organisation d'un colloque à Kathmandou en septembre 2009. L'analyse des nouvelles données se poursuit.

  7. Languages in contact with Tibeto-Burman languages: 2. Austro-Asiatic Languages (AA)
    The group of languages labelled Khasi, Khasic or Khasian, now spoken mostly in Meghalaya, a refuge land, but also in Assam, in India and in Bangladesh, has four non-mutually understandable languages: Pnar, Khasi, War and Lyngngam. Khasi appears to be a sub-branch of Pnaric. Pnar has been the lingua franca of a rather important kingdom for at least four centuries (before and during the Ahom kingdom) before the British colonisation imposed a written Standard Khasi as a lingua Franca. This situation has resulted in a number of mixed languages which might look at first glance like khasi dialects. War and Lyngngam appear to be issued from two different groups who joined the pnaric districts (doloiships) in Meghalaya rather recently and I call this group Pnaric-War-Lyngngam (PWL). Dialectal maps of Pnaric and War with their mixed languages are presented here and a detailed dialectal map of Lyngngam is in progress.

    As opposed to Khasi and to most modern AA languages, Pnar, War and Lyngngam did not develop any kind of verbal agreement with one or several arguments. A reference grammar of War in preparation and a chapter on War to appear show that the isolating morpho-syntax of War is centred on a system of polyfunctional particles made into a hierarchy which grammaticalize a number of pragmatic or discursive values. Utterances are not grounded with tense-aspect-mood markers but with particles referring to the subjectivity of the interlocutors and to aspect. Factual information is expressed without declarative particles. War has four salience particles focalizing several pecular agent and patient semantic roles, eventually roles involving either a subject or an object. War has no case marking and arguments are not marked unless salient. War has an auxiliary system without voice which expresses a gradiance of active and passive values (see Daladier 2012). Lexical predicates have selection restrictions, particles also have combinatorial restrictions and the two types of constraints interact. This complex morpho-syntactic system expresses propositional dependency without conjunctions (except borrowings) either by means of declarative particles dependencies or by means of lexicalized Serial Verb Constructions. This system structures in its own way an opposition between simple and complex utterances. This system seems to have an AA origin as vestiges are found in Northern and South-Eastern languages of the AA area like Khmuic and Aslian languages.
