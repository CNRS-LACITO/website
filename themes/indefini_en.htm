Indefinites


 

Coord. Isabelle Bril and Samia Naïm

 

While definite entities make up referential and shared knowledge, indefinite entities are generally defined as being non-referential (or not yet referential), their reference needs to be encoded and construed through indexical and situational clues.

The contrast between definiteness and indefiniteness is far from being universally encoded in the world's languages.
This research on indefinites has comparative and typological aims. It seeks to delineate the constructions and morphemes marking indefiniteness (determiners, numerals, quantifiers, nouns, pronouns, etc.). According to the World Atlas of Languages (WALS), only 40% of languages have an indefinite article ; what other strategies are at work ?
Scales and degrees of indefiniteness will be explored as well as relationship with specificity, genericity, free choice and their morphosyntactic expression.

Various types of correlations will be examined :

  • Number and gender : both are known to interact in expressing indefiniteness, contrasting for instance a singular indefinite article (often a numeral 'one') with a Ø morpheme in the plural.
  • Indefiniteness, mood and modality : non-specific indefinite entities, or entities with uncertain existence (unknown, unwitnessed) often associate with irrealis mood and epistemic modality.
  • Indefiniteness and verbal valency : indefiniteness is known to affect verbal valency and argument structure. In some languages, non-specific indefinite patients are "incorporated", the verb is then intransitive or intransitivised.
  • Relations between definiteness, specificity and informational hierarchy : for instance, it is well-known that only definite terms may be topicalised, and that other strategies must be used otherwise.

From the diachronic side, the origins and grammaticalisation processes of indefinite morphemes will be investigated, as well as their demise and the emergence of new markers.
