Language, Culture and the Environment

This group carries on the approaches developed by A.-G. Haudricourt and J.M.C. Thomas in ethnolinguistics, which grant equal importance to the three interdependent axes of language, sociocultural phenomena and the natural environment.
Considering language as both an instrument of communication and as a social phenomenon, the language of a given group is studied through analyzing its structures and mechanisms while also taking into account the natural and sociocultural environments in which it is spoken. This approach leads to analyzing the way in which a language organizes, classifies and appropriates the facts and objects found in its surroundings, but also leads to a deeper understanding and broader explanations of linguistic events through knowledge of the natural and sociocultural contexts within which language takes on meaning. Language is also approached in its dynamic aspect because, despite an unseverable past, it continues to evolve in close relation with the conception its speakers have of the world, and the representations, ideologies, beliefs, etc. which shape their views.
To properly analyze the close links between these different domains, this theme aims at interdisciplinarity. It groups together more than a dozen specialists in linguistics (description, comparison, typology), ethnolinguistics (oral literature, lexicography), ethnologists and sociolinguists (kinship systems, identity, representational systems) and ethnosciences (nomenclatures, taxonomies, representations, knowledge, know-how), covering various geographical areas: Africa, the Americas, the Arctic, Asia, Europe.
