Colloque "Steppes" - Workshop Steppes

29-30 septembre et 1er octobre 2007, Porquerolles (Var, France)
coord. F. Jacquesson

– 1st circular –

Dear Colleague,
Cher(e) collègue,

You are invited to a multi-disciplinary workshop “Steppes and their suburbs”, about the human population settling and movements in the Eurasian steppes.
The workshop is an offshoot from the “East meets West” project within ESF (Eurocores) Programme “Origin of Man, of Languages, of Language” (see www.esf.org/, Eurocores programmes, OMLL).

Place and date : Île de Porquerolles (Var, France),
Saturday 29- Sunday 30 September and Monday 1st October 2007.

Scientific Rationale
The purpose of this workshop is to have a better understanding of the problems posed by the history of nomadic herders on the whole of the ‘steppe corridor’, from the Magyar plains to Manchuria. The problems are historical, anthropological, linguistic and genetic. We intend to highlight the problems of deep time and long distance; contributions about local cultures will be taken into account when they help (they often do) to understand how things have been going on more generally. More often than not, these people have been considered as ‘without history’, or brutally disrupting the history of others: their history is either too cold or too hot. Can we imagine “a lukewarm steppe” ?

The mobility often taken as typical of these populations is, as we know, a far more complicated phenomenon than what the Romantic view saw as Völkerwanderung. First because massive movements were not the only ones, and most of the time not army-like. Secondly because the identification process within these populations and cultures is different according to conditions, or pliable enough to suffer a variety of conditions : the label(s) given to the group, as historians, anthropologists and linguists know well, is never simple, even if modern states like Russia and China are constantly tempted to consider these groups as ‘units’ (and consequently ‘minorities’) on the same model as themselves, only smaller and less advanced.

It may be the case now, but it was obviously different before, and population movements always re-modeled the previous equilibrium, either because only part of the population moved, or because different parts moved differently, or because the movement changed the identities by changing either the way of life or the alliances, and the way these people saw themselves. Names of ‘tribes’ have been changing unexpectedly or, still more embarrassing when one relies on names, have been re-appearing here and there. ‘Tradition’ seems to be a way of managing identities.

Moreover, it is now well-known that exchanges (goods, slaves, places) have been going on always between more mobile groups and more settled ones (‘the urban south’); it is likely that, sometimes at least, the settled and the mobile people were of the same families. The vision of elusive tribes herding in the wild, while quite distinct groups were protected within walls is probably more a cultural resassessment – dating from the Long Wall (or Limes) phenomenon – than a constant truth.

Archaeologists working in those places, either to the north of the Black Sea where references may come from Herodotus, or in Central Asia where the Arabic/Persian historians are the providers, or on the Eastern side with the Chinese Annals in hand, know how easily names such as Sarmatians or Xiongnu come to the tip of the tongue, and how difficult it actually is to ascribe such names to tombs or remains. Tombs do not speak, and we know that “Mongol armies” did not speak Mongol so much, so that the language supposed for the elite of a federation or empire is almost certainly not the same which the rank and file was speaking.

This workshop would like, not only to reaffirm these simple views against recurrent simplifications, but also to compare the experience of historians, archaeologists, anthropologists, linguists and geneticists who have been working in this huge area. We would like participants to choose in their experience illuminating cases in order to improve the ideas we may entertain, either because we work in another discipline, or because we work in a different place. For instance : How far is the Chinese Long Wall comparable to the Roman Limes ? How similar are the invaders of Gengis Khan to those associated with Tamerlan or Attila? Was the social or economical background the same? Is climatic history a sufficient explanation for the East-to-West movement of herders? How far can we figure out common cultural features for all (or parts) of these peoples? Are their common points only a fantasy born elsewhere and for aims quite alien to the ‘steppes’? These are only examples among the many questions that arose from a panoramic study of the identities in the “world of the steppes”.

The Workshop : time and place

The worshop will take place on the island of Porquerolles (Var departement), which is in the Mediterranean sea, south of Hyères. Access to the island will be by boat, but organized, for those who wish it, from the Toulon railway station by bus, and then by boat from Hyères.
About 25 speakers are expected. The workshop will take 3 full days, the 29-30 September and 1st October 2007, and speakers are requested to arrive on the island September the 28th in the evening.

The workshop is organized around 3 areas :

Day 1 : The West
Day 2 : The East
Day 3 : The Middle
