Colloque "Migration" - Workshop Migration

5-7 septembre 2007, Porquerolles (Var, France)
coord. F. Jacquesson
– 1st circular –

Dear Colleague,
Cher(e) collègue,

I have the pleasure to invite you to a Eurocores (European Science Foundation) workshop on What is a Migration ? to be held September 5-6-7, 2007, in Porquerolles Island, France.

Scientific background

Migrations entered the scientific scene in the romantic era, when it was believed, mainly for nationalistic purposes, that "peoples" roamed through lands or seas and eventually "settled". This racialist scenario is still obvious in maps where big sized arrows point from Somewhere to definite and sensitive places, never doubting that the presumed entity that moved was essentially identical through the movement. The typical (and in a large measure proto-typical) event being the Eurasian Dark Ages Migrations (the German Völkerwanderung, in French Grandes Invasions).

But migrations are not like that. They are, if we at all stick to the label ‘migration’, far more complex, in their demographic reality and economic and cultural explanations and meanings. The Barbarians in Eurasia, be it the north of the Roman Empire (with the limes), of the Iranian kingdoms, of the Chinese empire (with the Great Wall), actually had close relationships through the borders, and often were participants in the ‘empires’ to their south. The rush, when it indeed occurred, has to be analyzed in demographic composition (only males?) and movements (receding?, to and fro?). The dynamics of movement in the populations ‘at rest’ are to be understood if one hopes to understand the ‘upheavals’. Finally, population movements often result in integration, while the name given to the result often blurs the contents.

Anthropologists and prehistorians know how difficult it is to define a culture. Utensils in tombs may follow the fashion of the enlightened or more powerful neighbours, blunting a complex landscape. Several inovations are known to have travelled wide and far. The Aramean alphabet travelled from the Mediterranean shores right through Asia to Manchuria, without Arameans ever invading the Manchus. Mongol names for harnessing are found in Finnish, without Mongols ever having populated Finland. There are migratory facts that do no trace demographic movements.

This workshop would like to invite scholars to describe enlightening cases of migrations, wherever in the world and in the period they find most interesting, in order to improve our knowledge of what we mean by "migration". And to help assembling fine-grained examples which would remind fellow researchers, or a wider audience, that migrations do exist, but are to be analysed with historical caution.

Historians and prehistorians, anthropologists and linguists, demographers and geographers, political scientists and population geneticists are all concerned by these thematic reflections - at once methodological and comparative - to which we invite.

ESF and OMLL

Organized as a networking activity within the European Science Foundation OMLL programme (Origin of Man, of Language and Languages, see www.esf.org at Eurocores, then Programmes), the workshop responds to a need which is often expressed: a need for a more fundamental reflection on one of the key concepts in the investigation of the "Origin of Man and Languages", by specialists from all the various disciplines concerned.

The Workshop : time and place

The worshop will take place on the island of Porquerolles (Var departement), which is in the Mediterranean sea, south of Hyères. Access to the island will be by boat, but organized, for those who wish it, from the Toulon railway station by bus, and then by boat from Hyères.
About 25 speakers are expected. The workshop will take 3 full days, the 5-6-7, September 2007, and speakers are requested to arrive on the island September the 4th in the evening.

The workshop is organized around 6 sub-topics :

Day 1 : Demography and Society – Islands
Day 2 : Migration and Diffusion – Mountains
Day 3 : Genetics and traces – Population and space
